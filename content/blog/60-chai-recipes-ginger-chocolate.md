For a while now I've been jotting down chai variations on scraps of paper, in notes apps, on the backs of receipts. Different households, different moods, different seasons — each one a small variation on the same idea: tea, spice, milk, warmth.

It's time to do something with all of that. So here's the plan: **60 Chai Recipes**, a book-length collection, one cup at a time. Some will be classics. Some will be experiments. A few might not work at all — and that's fine, because the point isn't perfection, it's exploration.

## Why 60?

Sixty felt right. Enough to cover a recipe a week for over a year, enough to range from the everyday to the unexpected, and enough to actually call it a body of work rather than a list. Every post under this project will document one recipe, what inspired it, and how it turned out.

## Recipe 1: Ginger Chocolate Chai

The first idea on the list combines two things that already work beautifully on their own — the sharp warmth of ginger, and the deep comfort of chocolate — into one cup.

This isn't a hot chocolate with tea thrown in. The chai base stays front and center; the chocolate rounds out the spice rather than overpowering it. Think of it as a dessert chai, the kind of thing you'd make on a cold evening when a plain cup doesn't feel like enough.

**Ingredients (1 cup)**

- 1 cup water
- ½ cup whole milk
- 1½ tsp loose leaf black tea (Assam works well here — it can stand up to the chocolate)
- 1-inch piece of fresh ginger, crushed or thinly sliced
- 1 tbsp unsweetened cocoa powder
- 1–2 tsp sugar or jaggery, to taste
- Optional: a small piece of dark chocolate, grated, for serving

**Method**

1. Bring the water to a boil with the ginger. Let it simmer for 2–3 minutes so the ginger has time to release its heat into the water.
2. Add the tea leaves and simmer for another 1–2 minutes.
3. Whisk in the cocoa powder until fully dissolved — do this before adding the milk, so it doesn't clump.
4. Add the milk and sugar, and bring everything back to a gentle boil, stirring occasionally.
5. Strain into a cup. If you want, finish with a few shavings of dark chocolate on top — they'll melt slightly into the foam.

**Notes**

The ginger does a lot of work here. It cuts through the richness of the chocolate and keeps the cup from feeling heavy. If you want it sharper, increase the ginger slightly rather than adding more spice — this recipe is meant to stay simple, just tea, ginger, chocolate, and milk.
