Masala chai is not a single recipe. It is a philosophy. Every household, every dhaba, every grandmother has her own version — and each one is correct.

What I can offer is a framework: the spices, their roles, and how to find your own balance.

## The Core Spices

### Cardamom (Elaichi)
The backbone of most masala chai blends. It brings floral, almost minty brightness. Use green cardamom pods — crush them lightly to release the seeds.

### Ginger (Adrak)
Fresh ginger adds heat and sharpness. Dried ginger powder (*sonth*) gives a different warmth — more diffuse, less pungent. Both are valid depending on the day.

### Cinnamon (Dalchini)
Use a small piece of Ceylon cinnamon for sweetness. Cassia cinnamon is bolder and more bitter — it can overpower, so use sparingly.

### Black Pepper (Kali Mirch)
Often overlooked but transformative. Even two or three peppercorns add a pleasant heat that lingers at the back of the throat.

### Cloves (Laung)
One clove is enough for a single cup. More and you're drinking clove tea with a side of chai.

## A Starter Ratio (per litre of water)

| Spice | Amount |
|---|---|
| Green cardamom | 6 pods |
| Fresh ginger | 4 thin slices |
| Cinnamon | 1 small stick |
| Black pepper | 4 corns |
| Cloves | 2 |

Add 3 tsp of strong Assam tea and 1 cup of whole milk. Simmer for 4–5 minutes after the milk goes in for a deep, full-bodied cup.

## Tuning to Your Taste

Keep a small notebook. Make a cup, jot down what you changed, note what you'd do differently. Within a few weeks you'll have a blend that is unmistakably yours.

That's the real art of masala chai — not mastering someone else's recipe, but finding your own.
